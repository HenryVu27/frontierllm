## Orientation {#orientation}

Meta's Llama line is the frontier reference point for *open-weights* models — released with weights, weight-tying-permitted licenses (with restrictions), and the most transparent training-recipe disclosure of any frontier lab. Whereas OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google withhold parameter counts and training recipes, Meta publishes them. The Llama 3.1 Herd of Models technical report (arXiv:2407.21783) is the most-cited single artefact in 2024 frontier-LLM pretraining; subsequent Llama 4 disclosures continue the pattern with reduced but still meaningful detail.

This chapter walks the Llama 3 dense line, the Llama 4 MoE family, and the disclosed training and post-training pipelines. Calibration: May 15, 2026.

<Callout variant="note" title="What 'open-weights' means here">
The Llama line ships with weights under the Meta Community License. The license permits commercial use up to a usage threshold (700M monthly active users), restricts use of outputs to train competing models, and is not OSI-compliant. The community treats Llama as "open weights" rather than "open source"; the distinction matters for production use and is part of Meta's strategic positioning.
</Callout>

## The Llama 3 line {#llama-3-line}

**Llama 3 (April 2024).** 8B and 70B dense transformers. Trained on 15T tokens with the standard transformer recipe (Module 0a), RoPE positional encoding (Module 0c), Grouped-Query Attention (Module 0b), SwiGLU MLP (Module 0e), and RMSNorm (Module 0d). The 70B variant reached competitive MMLU at ~82%; the 8B variant established the new small-model frontier at ~66%.

**Llama 3.1 (July 2024).** Added the **405B** flagship — the largest open-weights model released to date — alongside 8B and 70B refreshes. The accompanying *Llama 3.1 Herd of Models* technical report disclosed: 15.6T training tokens, 16K H100 GPUs, 30.84M GPU-hours for pretraining (and a similar order for post-training), BF16 mixed-precision pretraining with FP8 used for quantization at inference. The 405B variant matched GPT-4 on most knowledge benchmarks (MMLU 87.3%, MATH 73.8%, HumanEval 89.0%, GPQA 50.7%) and substantially shifted the open-weights frontier.

Context extension: Llama 3.1 supports 128K tokens via staged long-context extension (Module 1g for the technique).

**Llama 3.2 (September 2024).** Multimodal expansion: 11B and 90B vision-language variants (text + image input) with a vision encoder bolted onto the base Llama 3.1 70B / Llama 3 11B. Small text-only variants at 1B and 3B for on-device deployment.

**Llama 3.3 (December 2024).** A 70B-only release that effectively replaced the 405B at the cost of much smaller inference: Meta reported the 70B variant matched 405B on most production benchmarks. The release implied — and the Llama 4 strategy confirmed — that pure dense scaling beyond 70B was producing diminishing returns relative to the inference cost.

The 3.x line is the canonical *dense* frontier model — no MoE, no mixture of capabilities, the classic Vaswani-Transformer recipe scaled. Module 1 (pretraining at scale) uses the 405B as its primary anchor for scaling-law calculations; this section is the released-artefact side.

## Llama 4 family {#llama-4-family}

The Llama 4 line (announced April 2025) is Meta's pivot to **Mixture of Experts** at frontier scale. Three variants:

**Llama 4 Scout (April 2025).** 109B total parameters, 17B activated per token, 16 experts, native 10M-token context. The 10M context — matching Gemini 1.5 — comes from a long-context training stage built on top of the base MoE. Scout is intended as the deployment workhorse of the line: low active-parameter count, frontier-class capability on knowledge benchmarks, very long context.

**Llama 4 Maverick (April 2025).** 400B total, 17B activated, 128 experts. Native multimodal (text + image input). Maverick alternates dense and MoE blocks — a hybrid architecture Meta argues balances inference efficiency (MoE) against representation quality (dense). Marketing: "best in class for multimodal".

**Llama 4 Behemoth (in training as of May 2025).** Reportedly ~2T total / ~288B activated, intended as a teacher model for distillation into Scout and Maverick rather than for production deployment. Behemoth's role echoes other labs' "frontier teacher / smaller student" patterns and the broader observation in Module 1 § 01h that very-large models can train better, smaller deployable models more reliably than the smaller models can train themselves.

The Llama 4 architectural choice has implications across the textbook: MoE routing and capacity (Module 0h, 02d), expert parallelism in distributed training (Module 2d), wide-EP inference deployment (Module 4h). Llama 4's expert count is mid-range — 16 in Scout, 128 in Maverick — versus DeepSeek-V3's auxiliary-loss-free MoE with substantially more experts. Meta has not published a head-to-head architectural comparison.

## Dense vs MoE strategy {#mixture-vs-dense-strategy}

The transition from Llama 3 (pure dense) to Llama 4 (MoE) is the strategic story of 2024–2025 for Meta. Two empirical motivations:

1. **Dense scaling ceiling.** Llama 3.1 405B and Llama 3.3 70B reaching parity on many production benchmarks signalled diminishing returns for pure dense scaling past ~500B parameters. Meta's internal MoE scaling-law experiments (referenced in the Llama 4 launch announcements but not fully published) showed ~16% efficiency advantage of MoE at frontier scale.
2. **Hardware alignment.** MoE matches the H100 / B200 hardware profile: sparse activation lets you scale total parameters without proportionally scaling active compute, exploiting interconnect bandwidth and memory hierarchy more effectively. Wide-expert-parallelism inference (Module 4h, DeepSeek-style) becomes the deployment pattern.

The trade-off Meta accepted: MoE training is more brittle than dense (auxiliary losses, load balancing, expert collapse risks — Module 0h). The Llama 4 launch indicates Meta judged the inference-efficiency gain worth the training-complexity cost.

The dense-to-MoE transition is the same architectural arc as DeepSeek (§ 06e) and Google's Gemini (§ 06c, MoE since 1.5). Llama 4 is Meta following the field's consensus on frontier architecture.

## Training recipe (405B) {#training-recipe-405b}

The Llama 3.1 405B training recipe is the most detailed frontier-model training disclosure published as of May 2026. Selected highlights from the *Herd of Models* technical report:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| **Total parameters** | 405B (dense) |
| **Training tokens** | 15.6T |
| **GPU type** | NVIDIA H100 |
| **GPU count** | 16,384 (16K) |
| **Pretraining GPU-hours** | 39.3M |
| **Precision** | BF16 (pretraining); FP8 (post-train quantization for inference) |
| **Context** | 8K base → 128K via long-context extension |
| **Architecture** | GQA, RoPE, SwiGLU, RMSNorm |
| **Tokenizer** | 128K vocabulary (vs Llama 2's 32K) |

The pretraining recipe followed the Chinchilla-compute-optimal trajectory (Module 1 § 01c), with inference-time overtraining beyond the Chinchilla point — a deliberate trade-off favouring inference cost over training cost (Module 1 § 01c on inference-aware scaling laws). FP8 was used in post-training quantization rather than pretraining itself; the contemporaneous DeepSeek-V3 line (§ 06e) was the first frontier model to use FP8 *during* pretraining.

**Parallelism configuration.** Llama 3.1 405B used a 4D parallelism stack: data parallel × tensor parallel × pipeline parallel × context parallel. The exact configuration (TP=8, PP=16, CP=4, DP=32, approximately) is documented in the technical report. Cross-reference Module 2 § 02h for the 3D / 4D parallelism composition.

**Data pipeline.** The Herd report describes the data pipeline (Module 1 § 01a) at length: multi-stage deduplication, quality filtering, multilingual rebalancing. Final pretraining data composition is approximately 50% web, 25% code, 12% math/reasoning, 8% multilingual, 5% other.

## Post-training pipeline {#post-training-pipeline}

The Llama 3.1 405B post-training pipeline replaced PPO-based RLHF with **iterative DPO** (Module 3 § 03b). The pipeline:

1. **SFT.** ~25M+ synthetic + human examples. Synthetic generation uses Llama 3.1 itself plus seed quality-filtered demonstrations.
2. **Reward modelling.** A reward model trained on pairwise preference data.
3. **Iterative DPO (2–4 rounds).** Each round: sample completions from the current policy, label with the reward model, run DPO. Re-initialize for the next round.
4. **Multi-objective fine-tuning.** Optimization against multiple reward signals (helpfulness, harmlessness, calibration, instruction-following).

Post-training GPU-hours: roughly 30.84M, an order of magnitude smaller than pretraining but still ~78% of pretraining cost — a high ratio reflecting Meta's investment in the post-training side of the recipe. The DPO-over-PPO choice (Module 3 § 03b on the algorithmic tradeoff) reflects Meta's reported finding that DPO is simpler to scale and equally effective at the 405B size.

## Open-weights licensing {#open-weights-license}

The **Meta Community License** governs the Llama weights. Key terms:

- **Commercial use permitted** up to 700M monthly active users (a threshold mostly affecting the largest tech companies).
- **Output restrictions.** Outputs cannot be used to train competing models. This is the term that disqualifies the license from OSI compliance and is the most-cited constraint in production use.
- **Attribution.** Required under most clauses.

The license is more permissive than most "open" model licenses (Mistral's MRL, Cohere's CC-BY-NC-4.0 for weights) and less permissive than truly open licenses (Apache 2.0, MIT). Meta's positioning: Llama weights are available enough to drive adoption and ecosystem development (Hugging Face, llama.cpp, Ollama, the Llama 3.1 405B base for downstream fine-tunes) without entirely commoditising frontier model capabilities. Gemma (§ 06c, Apache 2.0) and DeepSeek (§ 06e, MIT) sit at the more-permissive end of the open-weights spectrum.

The Llama community-license terms are the same across Llama 3 and Llama 4; updates have been minor.

## Summary {#summary}

Meta's Llama line is the open-weights pillar of the frontier ecosystem. The Llama 3 line (April 2024 – December 2024) was the canonical dense-Transformer recipe scaled to 405B and detailed in the most cited frontier-model technical report of 2024. The Llama 4 line (April 2025 onward) pivots to MoE — Scout (109B/17B, 10M context), Maverick (400B/17B, multimodal), Behemoth (~2T/288B, teacher) — joining DeepSeek and Gemini at the MoE frontier.

Cross-references for a reader of earlier modules: pretraining recipe ↔ Module 1 throughout (with § 01h annealing/FP8 as the closest specific cross-ref); 4D parallelism ↔ Module 2 § 02h; iterative DPO post-training ↔ Module 3 § 03b; MoE architecture ↔ Module 0h, Module 2d; multimodal vision encoders ↔ Module 0g (extended). Frontier-lab strategic comparison: Llama is the most-disclosed frontier line, the most-permissively-licensed of the major frontier labs, and now the most architecturally heterogeneous Western open-weights line.

Two empirical anchors as of May 15 2026: Llama 3.1 405B (the canonical reference dense model, 87.3% MMLU, with the most detailed published recipe) and Llama 4 Scout (the production deployment workhorse with frontier-class 10M context at 17B active parameters). Open questions: Llama 4 Behemoth's release timeline and disclosed details, full third-party verification of Llama 4 benchmark claims, and the exact MoE-routing policy used in Maverick.
