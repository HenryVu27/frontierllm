## About this module {#about}

Modules 0–4 cover the *building* of frontier large language models: architecture, pretraining, distributed training, post-training, inference and serving. This module covers the *measurement and understanding* of them. It is the textbook's treatment of the third arm of the frontier-LLM lifecycle: how do we know what a model can do, how do we read what it is doing internally, and how do we keep it doing what we intended?

After the eight chapters, a reader should be able to take any 2024–2026 evaluation paper, interpretability blog post, or safety report and place every claim into a coherent map: which benchmark family, which oversight protocol, which interpretability technique, which red-team or deception-evaluation methodology.

This module does **not** teach the training-side material (Modules 0–3) or the serving-side material (Module 4); cross-references point back to those modules whenever a method appears. The next planned module (Module 6) covers the frontier-lab case studies — the artefacts that consume everything in Modules 0–5.

<Callout variant="note" title="Calibration date">
Content is calibrated to frontier eval, alignment, and interpretability practice as of May 15, 2026. The conceptual frameworks (sandwiching, debate, sufficiency-vs-necessity, contamination detection methodology) are durable. The benchmark leaderboards, the specific Arena methodology, the SAE training recipes, and the published red-teaming protocols age fastest. The deceptive-alignment evaluation literature is the most volatile section — both the threat model and the protocols are under active development.
</Callout>

## What this module covers {#chapter-list}

**[Chapter 46 — Benchmark families for frontier LLMs](/textbook/05a-benchmarks)**
Knowledge (MMLU, GPQA, HLE), reasoning (MATH, AIME, FrontierMath, ARC-AGI-2), code (HumanEval, LiveCodeBench, SWE-bench), long-context (RULER), multimodal (MMMU-Pro, MathVista), and agentic (GAIA, BrowseComp) benchmark families. The 2024–2026 saturation timeline and the move to expert-authored, dynamic, and composite scoring.

**[Chapter 47 — Test-set contamination](/textbook/05b-contamination)**
N-gram and embedding-based detection. Min-K% probability detection (Shi 2023). Paraphrase contamination. LiveBench's monthly-refreshed protocol. The 2025 ICML result that no mitigation strategy achieves both fidelity and contamination-resistance.

**[Chapter 48 — LMSYS Chatbot Arena and ELO methodology](/textbook/05c-arena-elo)**
Bradley-Terry-Luce derivation. Pairwise preference at scale. Style-controlled ELO. Arena-Hard-Auto. The Llama-4 Maverick gaming incident. The 2025 "Leaderboard Illusion" critique.

**[Chapter 49 — Sparse autoencoders](/textbook/05d-saes)**
Bricken 2023 (Toy Models of Superposition, Monosemanticity). Templeton 2024 (Scaling Monosemanticity, Golden Gate Claude). Gemma Scope. TopK / JumpReLU / BatchTopK / Matryoshka variants. Feature absorption and splitting pathologies.

**[Chapter 50 — Circuit analysis](/textbook/05e-circuit-analysis)**
Induction heads (Olsson 2022), the IOI circuit (Wang 2022), activation and attribution patching (Nanda 2023; Heimersheim 2024), function vectors (Todd 2023), transcoders (Dunefsky 2024), sparse-feature circuits (Marks 2024), and the 2025 attribution-graph programme (Lindsey, Anthropic).

**[Chapter 51 — Scalable oversight](/textbook/05f-scalable-oversight)**
Debate (Irving 2018), iterative amplification (Christiano 2018), recursive reward modelling (Leike 2018), weak-to-strong generalisation (Burns 2023), deliberative alignment (Guan 2024), and the sandwiching empirical programme.

**[Chapter 52 — Red-teaming and jailbreaks](/textbook/05g-red-teaming)**
Automated red-teaming (Perez 2022), GCG (Zou 2023), PAIR (Chao 2023), AutoDAN, many-shot jailbreaking, HarmBench, JailbreakBench, and the Anthropic ASL / OpenAI Preparedness / DeepMind FSF pre-deployment frameworks.

**[Chapter 53 — Deceptive alignment evaluations](/textbook/05h-deceptive-alignment-evals)**
Sleeper Agents (Hubinger 2024), Alignment Faking (Greenblatt 2024), Apollo Research scheming evaluations, agentic-misalignment benchmarks, and the Situational Awareness Dataset.

## Notation conventions {#notation}

- $\mathcal{D}$ — evaluation dataset; $|\mathcal{D}|$ — its cardinality.
- $\text{Acc}(\pi, \mathcal{D})$ — accuracy of policy $\pi$ on $\mathcal{D}$.
- $r_\theta(x, y)$ — reward model; $\pi_\phi$ — policy under evaluation.
- $\text{ELO}(\pi)$ — Bradley-Terry-Luce rating for policy $\pi$.
- $f_i$ — sparse-autoencoder feature $i$; $a_i$ — its activation.
- $\Delta(\ell)$ — patching effect at location $\ell$.
- $C$ — written constitution / specification.
- $\text{PGR}$ — performance gap recovered (weak-to-strong generalisation).
- $\alpha$ — attack success rate (red-teaming).
- Module-wide notation inherits from Modules 0–4: $N$ (parameters), $D$ (tokens), $L$ (layers), $H$ (heads), $d_h$ (head dim), $\pi$ (policy), $r$ (reward).

## Reading order recommendations {#reading-order}

### First time through
Chapters 46–53 in order. Evaluation methodology (46–48) → interpretability (49–50) → alignment protocols (51) → safety stress-tests (52–53). The dependency is conceptual: you need a benchmark vocabulary before you can read a safety report.

### For a research engineer focused on alignment
Chapters 51, 52, 53, then 46–48 for the eval-methodology background. The alignment protocols make most sense when you already know what scalable oversight is trying to scale past.

### For an interpretability researcher
Chapters 49, 50, 53. SAEs and circuit analysis are the technical core; deceptive-alignment evaluations are where the interpretability programme is most stress-tested.

### For an evaluation engineer
Chapters 46, 47, 48, with side-glances at 52 (red-teaming benchmarks). Saturation, contamination, and pairwise-preference statistics are the production concerns.

### For a safety / governance reader
Chapters 52, 53, 51, then 46–48. Pre-deployment safety frameworks, deception evaluations, and scalable-oversight protocols are the load-bearing concepts; benchmarks are the measurement substrate.

## What success looks like {#success-criteria}

After completing the module a reader should be able to:

1. Choose a benchmark family appropriate to a capability claim, and recognise contamination, saturation, and reward-hacking failure modes.
2. Read an Arena leaderboard score and articulate what it does and does not measure.
3. Identify SAE features and circuits in a published interpretability paper, and judge the evidential strength of the causal claims.
4. Describe a scalable-oversight protocol (debate, amplification, weak-to-strong, deliberative alignment) and argue about its tradeoffs.
5. Design a red-team protocol against a frontier model, including automated attacks and the responsible-disclosure process.
6. Read a deceptive-alignment evaluation and place its threat model into the published taxonomy.
